What Is a Sanad? Goa's Land-Conversion Certificate Explained

July 2026·6 min read ·Mindscape Properties

If you plan to build in Goa, one document decides whether you legally can: the Sanad. Here's what a Sanad is, why agricultural land needs converting, how it differs from zoning, and how to get — or verify — one.

Key Takeaways
  • A Sanad is the order converting land from agricultural to non-agricultural (settlement) use in Goa.
  • Without a Sanad, building on agricultural land is illegal and un-financeable — and hard to resell.
  • Sanad and zoning are different: zoning permits building; the Sanad converts the plot's recorded use.
  • Always verify the Sanad against the survey number and Form I & XIV — never rely on a verbal 'it's convertible'.

Ask anyone who has bought land in Goa what nearly tripped them up, and one word comes up again and again: Sanad. It sounds bureaucratic, and it is — but it is also the single document that decides whether you can legally build a home on a plot. If you take one thing from our land guides, take this.

So, what is a Sanad?

A Sanad is the official order that converts land from agricultural use to non-agricultural (settlement) use. In India, agricultural land is meant for farming — you cannot simply build a house on it. Before construction is legally permitted, the land's recorded use must be changed, and in Goa that conversion order is called a Sanad. It is issued by the revenue authorities under the Goa Land Revenue Code, and it comes with the payment of conversion charges.

In short: no Sanad, no legal building on land that is recorded as agricultural.

Why it matters so much

Buyers are often told a plot is 'ready to build' or that conversion is 'just a formality we'll finish later'. Treat that with caution. Building on unconverted agricultural land can mean:

  • No occupancy certificate — and therefore trouble getting water, power and a legal completion.
  • Illegal construction exposed to penalties, stop-work orders or even demolition.
  • A broken resale — future buyers (and their lawyers and banks) will refuse a plot without a clean Sanad.
  • Blocked home loans — lenders typically will not finance construction on unconverted land.
A plot without a Sanad is farmland with a view — not a building site. The certificate is what turns one into the other.Mindscape Properties

Sanad vs zoning — they are not the same thing

This is the most common confusion, so it's worth being precise. You need both, and they answer different questions:

  • Zoning (under the Regional Plan and Outline Development Plan) decides *whether* a plot is allowed to be built on at all — settlement, orchard, No-Development or eco-sensitive zone.
  • The Sanad is the *conversion* of that specific plot's recorded use from agricultural to non-agricultural, once the zoning permits it.

Zoning has to permit building before a Sanad can follow. Land in a No-Development or eco-sensitive zone generally cannot be converted at all — which is exactly why a suspiciously cheap plot needs both checks, not one.

How a Sanad is obtained

The broad process, which the current owner (or the buyer, once they own it) applies for through the revenue department:

  • Application to the Collector / Deputy Collector for conversion under the Goa Land Revenue Code, with the title documents, Form I & XIV and the survey plan.
  • Verification & NOCs — the authorities confirm zoning, and clearances may be required from bodies such as the town planning department and, near the coast, CRZ authorities.
  • Payment of conversion charges — a one-time fee calculated on the area and use.
  • Issue of the Sanad — the formal conversion order, after which construction can be legally sanctioned.

Timelines and charges vary and change with policy, so confirm the current position with a local property lawyer before you rely on any figure.

How to check whether a plot already has a Sanad

When you view land, ask the seller directly for the Sanad and verify it — don't take a verbal assurance:

  • Ask for the Sanad / conversion order itself and check the survey number matches the plot.
  • Cross-check the land's recorded use in Form I & XIV and the revenue records.
  • Have a lawyer confirm the Sanad is valid, for the intended use, and not lapsed or subject to conditions.
  • If there is no Sanad, treat conversion as a condition of purchase — not an afterthought — and budget the time and cost.

A Sanad is not red tape to fear — it is the document that protects your right to build and your resale value. It sits at the heart of our land-buying checklist and our wider due-diligence process. Verify it early, and a plot becomes a genuine building opportunity rather than a gamble.

Frequently Asked

What is a Sanad in Goa?

A Sanad is the official conversion order that changes a plot's recorded use from agricultural to non-agricultural (settlement) use under the Goa Land Revenue Code. It is required before a home can be legally built on land recorded as agricultural, and it involves paying conversion charges.

Can I build a house on agricultural land in Goa without a Sanad?

No. Building on agricultural land without first obtaining a Sanad (and where the zoning permits conversion) is illegal. It typically results in no occupancy certificate, penalties or demolition risk, blocked home loans, and serious resale problems.

Is a Sanad the same as zoning approval?

No. Zoning, under the Regional Plan and Outline Development Plan, determines whether a plot may be built on at all. The Sanad converts that specific plot's recorded use from agricultural to non-agricultural. You need the zoning to permit building before a Sanad can be granted, so both must be checked.
